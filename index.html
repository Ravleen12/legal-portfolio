Contract Law

Tap to flip

Real-World Case

Lucy v. Zehmer — “Farm on a Receipt”

The Case: Two men signed a contract to sell a farm on a restaurant receipt. One later claimed he was “just joking.”

The Clarity: If you act like you are serious, the law says you are. Contracts are about outward actions, not secret thoughts.

Corporate

“ … a business corporation is organized and carried on primarily for the profit of the stockholders. The powers of the directors are to be employed for that end. The discretion of directors is to be exercised in the choice of means to attain that end … ”

Tap to flip

Real-World Case

Dodge v. Ford

The Case: Henry Ford wanted to use profits to help employees rather than pay dividends to shareholders.

The Clarity: Corporations exist primarily to make money for their owners (shareholders), not just for general social good.

Litigation

Real-World Case

Liebeck v. McDonald’s — Hot Coffee

The Case: A woman sued over “hot coffee” burns.

The Clarity: This wasn’t a “frivolous” case; it was about a company serving coffee at 190°F (which causes 3rd-degree burns) and refusing to fix the safety hazard.

Real-World Case

“Happy Birthday” Song Copyright

The Agreement: A company claimed they owned the copyright to the song “Happy Birthday.”

The Clarity: The court ruled the song belongs to the public. You can’t charge people a “royalty” for a song that has been around so long it belongs to everyone.

Milestones in Clarity

Key moments in legal history that made the law more accessible.

1963

Gideon v. Wainwright

The Supreme Court ruled that states must provide lawyers to criminal defendants who cannot afford one—making legal representation a right, not a privilege.

1970s

The Plain English Movement

Lawyers and consumer advocates began pushing for clearer legal language in contracts, government forms, and consumer documents.

1966

Miranda v. Arizona

Police must inform people of their rights in plain language—the origin of “You have the right to remain silent”—so people can actually understand and use them.

2010

Plain Writing Act

U.S. federal law requiring agencies to use clear, straightforward language the public can understand—legalese is no longer acceptable by default.
